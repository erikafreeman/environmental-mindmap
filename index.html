Co-benefits Focus: Founded by WWF, Gold Standard issued 44M credits in 2021, focusing on social & community impacts. Unlike Verra, GS has proportionally fewer nature-based projects (only 4% of credits). Emphasizes sustainable development goals alongside emission reductions.

üü¢ American Carbon Registry (ACR)

First Private Registry: Operating since 1996, ACR is the first private registry in the world for voluntary carbon markets, geographically focused on the Americas including Canada and LATAM.

üî¥ Climate Action Reserve (CAR)

US-Focused Standard: CAR grew out of a database created by the state of California. CAR credits can be transferred to VCS, so CAR-issued credits can be converted into VCUs.

üèÜ Integrity Council Approval

CCP Label: In 2024, the ICVCM approved five registries: ACR, Architecture for REDD+ Transactions, CAR, Gold Standard, and Verra for Core Carbon Principles (CCP) labeling. This provides buyers with additional quality assurance.

üìú Kyoto Protocol (1997)

First Global Carbon Market: Established binding emission reduction commitments for developed countries and created three flexible mechanisms: Emissions Trading, Joint Implementation, and the Clean Development Mechanism (CDM). The CDM allowed industrialized countries to invest in emission reduction projects in developing countries, generating Certified Emission Reduction credits (CERs).

üîÑ Clean Development Mechanism

Project-Based Crediting: As of March 2024, 1,389 CDM projects and 119 programmes of activities have filed documents to transition into the Article 6.4 system, potentially creating nearly 1 billion repackaged CDM credits. However, concerns exist about credit quality from legacy projects.

üèõÔ∏è UNFCCC Framework

Global Climate Governance: The United Nations Framework Convention on Climate Change provides the overarching framework for international climate cooperation, including the development of market mechanisms under both Kyoto and Paris agreements.

Key Concepts:

  • Annex I Countries: Developed nations with emission reduction obligations
  • Non-Annex I Countries: Developing nations eligible to host CDM projects
  • Joint Implementation: Emission reduction projects between Annex I countries
  • Emission Trading: Cap-and-trade systems for carbon allowances
ü§ù Paris Agreement & Article 6
+
Next-generation carbon markets - COP29 finalized Article 6 rules, paving the way for a new era in global carbon markets with first trades expected by end of 2025.
üéØ Article 6.2 - Bilateral Trading

Country-to-Country Trading: Article 6.2 allows countries to trade emission reductions through bilateral agreements. These traded credits are called Internationally Transferred Mitigation Outcomes (ITMOs). Japan and Indonesia are already preparing bilateral agreements.

Requirements:

  • Host country authorization
  • Corresponding adjustments in national accounts
  • Robust accounting to prevent double counting
  • Sustainable development co-benefits
üè¢ Article 6.4 - Centralized Mechanism

UN-Supervised Trading: The Paris Agreement Crediting Mechanism (PACM) replaces the CDM under UN oversight. Credits are called Article 6.4 Emission Reductions ('6.4 ERs'). This provides a centralized alternative to bilateral trading.

‚öñÔ∏è Corresponding Adjustments

Anti-Double Counting: Host countries must discount abatement from national accounts when transferring credits, ensuring units aren't counted by more than one party. Critical for CORSIA compliance.

üö® COP29 Breakthrough

Implementation Ready: COP29 resolved key issues including revocation clauses and CDM credit transfers. Once ITMOs have been "first transferred," they cannot be affected by revocations unless specified by Parties.

üìä Market Mechanics
+
How carbon credits are traded and tracked - Understanding the infrastructure and processes that enable carbon credit transactions from issuance to retirement.
üîÑ Credit Lifecycle

From Project to Retirement: Credits move through project registration, validation, issuance, trading in secondary markets, and final retirement when used for offsetting claims.

Lifecycle Stages:

  • Registration: Project approved by standards body
  • Validation: Third-party assessment of project design
  • Issuance: Credits generated and entered into registry
  • Trading: Credits bought/sold in secondary markets
  • Retirement: Credits permanently removed from circulation
üíπ Trading Platforms

Market Infrastructure: ACX (AirCarbon Exchange) won best carbon exchange in 2024. Following steep price drops in November 2023, the market has remained relatively flat with most credits trading below $2.

üö´ Double Counting Prevention

Registry Controls: Registries maintain unique serial numbers and track ownership changes. Credits are "retired" to represent permanent removal and single use, ensuring the same emissions unit cannot be used more than once.

üìà Price Dynamics

Market Factors: Average vintage age rose to 6.1 years in 2021, with retirement of older credits including avoided deforestation projects over ten years old driving this trend. Removal projects command price premiums due to permanence characteristics.

üí∞ Economic Theory
+
Theoretical foundations of carbon markets - Economic principles that underpin market-based environmental solutions.
üìö Pigou Tax Theory (1920)

Internalizing Externalities: Arthur Pigou proposed imposing taxes on polluting activities to internalize social costs, making polluters pay for environmental damage. This formed the basis for carbon pricing mechanisms.

üîÑ Coase Theorem (1960)

Property Rights Solution: Ronald Coase proposed creating transferable property rights for pollution as a market-based solution, forming the theoretical foundation for emissions trading systems and carbon markets.

üí∏ Transaction Cost Analysis

Market Efficiency: Transaction costs significantly impact market participation. NGOs often find voluntary markets more suitable due to smaller project scales and lower transaction costs compared to compliance markets.

Key Economic Concepts:

  • Externalities: Environmental costs not reflected in market prices
  • Price Discovery: Market mechanisms to find efficient carbon pricing
  • Additionality: Ensuring projects wouldn't happen without carbon finance
  • Marginal Abatement Cost: Cost of reducing one additional unit of emissions

üîª Reduction Projects

Avoiding Emissions: Projects that prevent greenhouse gas emissions from being released into the atmosphere. Include renewable energy, energy efficiency, and waste management projects.
